Course Description

This course provides a comprehensive introduction to solid state physics. The course focuses on crystal structure, lattice dynamics, electronic band theory, electron transport in metals and semiconductors, magnetism, superconductivity, and experimental methods for studying solids. Special attention is paid to the microscopic physical mechanisms underlying macroscopic properties of solid materials.

Lecture Topics and Questions

Part 1. Crystal Lattice Geometry

Lecture 1. Introduction to Solid State Physics. Crystal Lattice Geometry

  1. Subject, experimental and theoretical methods of solid state physics. Role of solid state physics in modern physical science.
  2. Crystalline and amorphous solids. Types of crystal lattices: Bravais lattice and lattice with a basis. Unit cell.
  3. Bravais lattices.
  4. Indices of lattice points, crystallographic directions and planes. Miller indices.

Lecture 2. Wave Propagation in Periodic Structures. Reciprocal Lattice

  1. Diffraction as the primary method for crystal structure determination. Wulff–Bragg law.
  2. Laue equations.
  3. Reciprocal lattice and its properties.
  4. Ewald construction.
  5. Brillouin zones.

Lecture 3. Types of Chemical Bonding in Crystals. Crystal Defects

  1. General characteristics of chemical bonding in solids:
    • ionic bonding;
    • covalent bonding;
    • metallic bonding;
    • molecular (van der Waals) bonding;
    • hydrogen bonding.
  2. Crystal defects:
    • point defects: Frenkel and Schottky defects;
    • line defects (dislocations);
    • surface defects;
    • volume defects.

Part 2. Band Theory of Solids

Lecture 4. Approximate Methods for Solving the Schrödinger Equation in Crystals

  1. Schrödinger equation for electrons in a crystal.
  2. Adiabatic approximation. Valence approximation.
  3. Hartree–Fock self-consistent field method (single-electron approximation).
  4. Bloch theorem.
  5. Qualitative analysis of the electronic energy spectrum. Formation of energy bands.
  6. Tight-binding approximation. Band formation and bandwidths for SC, BCC, and FCC lattices.

Lecture 5. Kronig–Penney Model. Band Filling

  1. Schrödinger equation with the periodic Kronig–Penney potential.
  2. Analysis of solutions:
    • weak electron–atom interaction;
    • strong electron–atom interaction.
  3. Filling of energy bands in mono-, di-, and multivalent materials. Conductors, semiconductors, and insulators.

Part 3. Crystal Lattice Dynamics

Lecture 6. Normal Vibrations of the Crystal Lattice. Quasiparticles

  1. Lattice vibrations. Harmonic and anharmonic effects. Phonons.
  2. Quasiparticle method in solid state physics. Examples of quasiparticles. Comparison between quasiparticles and real particles.
  3. Acoustic and optical vibrations:
    • one-dimensional monatomic lattice;
    • one-dimensional diatomic lattice.
  4. Anharmonicity and thermal expansion.

Lecture 7. Phonon Density of States. Lattice Heat Capacity

  1. Phonon density of states. Debye frequency and Debye temperature.
  2. Experimental data and classical theory of lattice heat capacity.
  3. Einstein theory of lattice heat capacity.
  4. Debye theory of lattice heat capacity.
  5. Electronic heat capacity.

Part 4. Electron Gas in Metals

Lecture 8. Electron Gas in Metals. Effective Mass. Boltzmann Equation

  1. Maxwell–Boltzmann, Fermi–Dirac, and Bose–Einstein statistics for a gas of quasiparticles.
  2. Chemical potential, Fermi energy, and Fermi level in metals.
  3. Electron as a quasiparticle. Effective mass and methods of its determination.
  4. Boltzmann kinetic equation for electrons in metals.

Lecture 9. Electrical Conductivity of Metals. Ohm’s Law

  1. Basic concepts of the classical theory of electrical conductivity in metals.
  2. Boltzmann equation in the relaxation-time approximation.
  3. Ohm’s law and its analysis.
  4. Comparison of experimental and theoretical results:
    • Bloch–Grüneisen law;
    • electron–phonon and electron–electron scattering; Matthiessen’s rule;
    • Kondo effect;
    • electron–magnon scattering.

Lecture 10. Thermal Conductivity of Metals

Part 5. Electronic Theory of Semiconductors

Lecture 11. Classification of Semiconductors. Carrier Statistics

  1. General characteristics of semiconductors.
  2. Intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors.
  3. Degeneracy. Degenerate and non-degenerate semiconductors.
  4. Equilibrium carrier concentration in intrinsic semiconductors. Generalized carrier statistics.
  5. Equilibrium carrier concentration in doped semiconductors.
  6. Temperature dependence of the chemical potential in intrinsic and extrinsic semiconductors.

Lecture 12. Electrical Conductivity of Semiconductors. Hall Effect

  1. Electrical conductivity of intrinsic semiconductors.
  2. Electrical conductivity of doped semiconductors.
  3. Determination of the energy band gap.
  4. Hall effect:
    • in metals;
    • in semiconductors.

Lecture 13. Contact and Thermoelectric Phenomena

  1. Metal–metal contact.
  2. Metal–semiconductor contact.
  3. p–n junction.
  4. Rectification of electric current.
  5. Thermoelectric effects.

Part 6. Microscopic Theories of Magnetism

Lecture 14. Diamagnetism and Paramagnetism

  1. General properties of magnetic materials. Curie and Néel temperatures.
  2. Elementary theory of diamagnetism.
  3. Classical Langevin theory of paramagnetism.
  4. Pauli paramagnetism and Landau diamagnetism.

Lecture 15. Theories of Ferromagnetism

  1. Weiss theory of ferromagnetism.
  2. Thermodynamic (Landau) theory of ferromagnetism.
  3. Exchange interaction in strongly magnetic materials.

Part 7. Superconductivity

Lecture 16. Superconductivity. BCS Theory

  1. Superconductivity phenomenon. Critical temperature.
  2. Meissner effect.
  3. Formation of Cooper pairs. Microscopic origin of superconductivity.

Part 8. Experimental Methods of Solid State Physics

Lecture 17. Experimental Methods in Solid State Physics

  1. Investigation of crystal structure, elemental and phase composition.
  2. Study of electronic structure and magnetic properties of crystals.
