Course Description

This course introduces the fundamental principles of statistical physics and statistical thermodynamics. The course develops the connection between microscopic dynamics and macroscopic thermodynamic behavior, covers Gibbs ensembles, classical and quantum statistics, ideal and interacting systems, fluctuations, kinetic theory, transport phenomena, and phase transitions.

Part 1. Fundamental Principles of Statistical Physics

Lecture 1. Introduction. Methods of Analytical Mechanics

  1. Subject, objectives, methods, and significance of statistical physics.
  2. Classification of physical systems. Types of interactions between particles.
  3. Virtual and real displacements. Principle of virtual work (equilibrium condition).
  4. Generalized coordinates and generalized forces.
  5. D’Alembert–Lagrange principle of virtual displacements.

Lecture 2. Lagrange and Hamilton Equations for Particle Systems

  1. Lagrange equations of motion.
  2. Lagrangian function and its relation to energy. Hamiltonian function. Generalized momenta.
  3. Lagrangian and Hamiltonian variables. Legendre transformation. Hamilton’s (canonical) equations of motion.

Lecture 3. Phase Space. Distribution Function

  1. Phase space concept. Ensemble and time averages. Distribution function over microstates. Ergodic and quasi-ergodic hypotheses.
  2. Liouville theorem. Properties of the distribution function.
  3. Features of quantum systems:
    • wave–particle duality;
    • discreteness of physical observables;
    • indistinguishability of particles, spin, Pauli exclusion principle;
    • Heisenberg uncertainty relations;
    • statistical weight (thermodynamic probability), degeneracy.

Lecture 4. Gibbs Distributions for Classical and Quantum Systems

  1. Definition and properties of the Dirac delta function and its applications.
  2. Microcanonical Gibbs distribution.
  3. Canonical Gibbs distribution. Partition function and statistical integral.
  4. Grand canonical Gibbs distribution.
  5. Relation between partition function and statistical integral. Physical meaning and properties of the canonical distribution.

Part 2. Statistical Distributions for Ideal Gases

Lecture 5. Maxwell and Boltzmann Distributions

  1. General Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution.
  2. Boltzmann distribution in an external potential. Barometric formula. Maxwell distribution of molecular speeds.
  3. Maxwell distribution of velocity components.
  4. Mean molecular speed.
  5. Root-mean-square molecular speed.
  6. Most probable molecular speed.

Lecture 6. Applications of Maxwell and Boltzmann Distributions

  1. Molecular flux to a surface. Gas pressure.
  2. Thermionic emission. Richardson equation.
  3. Contact potential difference: metal–metal, semiconductor–semiconductor, metal–semiconductor systems.
  4. Distribution of molecules in a centrifuge.
  5. Planetary atmospheres.

Part 3. Foundations of Statistical Thermodynamics

Lecture 7. Statistical Foundations of Thermodynamic Laws

  1. Phenomenological and statistical thermodynamics. Internal energy of a macroscopic system.
  2. Quasistatic processes. External parameters. Work and heat from the statistical point of view. First law of thermodynamics.
  3. Entropy in statistical physics. Gibbs theorem. Entropy of nonequilibrium systems. Entropy increase law. Second law of thermodynamics.
  4. Statistical interpretation of the second law of thermodynamics.
  5. Statistical foundation of the third law of thermodynamics.

Lecture 8. Thermodynamic Potentials

  1. Liquid–vapor phase transition.
  2. Method of thermodynamic potentials. Internal energy, free energy, chemical potential, grand potential.
  3. Calculation of pressure, volume, temperature, entropy. First and second groups of Maxwell relations.
  4. Fundamental thermodynamic equation for systems with variable particle number.
  5. Statistical calculation of thermodynamic potentials.

Lecture 9. Thermodynamic Properties of Classical Ideal Gases

  1. Equipartition theorem.
  2. Classical theory of heat capacity of ideal gases.
  3. Quantum theory of heat capacity of ideal gases.

Lecture 10. Interacting Particle Systems. Quantum Statistics

  1. Distribution of particles over quantum states. Grand potential of a quantum state and macroscopic system.
  2. Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics.
  3. Fermi–Dirac statistics.
  4. Bose–Einstein statistics.
  5. Degenerate and non-degenerate systems. Degeneracy temperature.

Lecture 11. Applications of Fermi–Dirac Statistics to Electron Systems

  1. Degenerate electron gas. Density of states. Fermi energy and Fermi temperature.
  2. Internal energy of an electron gas at absolute zero and its temperature dependence.
  3. Heat capacity of an electron gas in metals at constant volume.

Lecture 12. Applications of Bose–Einstein Statistics to Boson Systems

  1. Theory of real gases. Van der Waals equation. Van der Waals isotherms. Critical parameters.
  2. Bose gas:
    • massive bosons and Bose–Einstein condensation;
    • photons as massless bosons. Theory of thermal radiation.

Part 4. Fluctuations

Lecture 13. Fluctuation Theory

  1. Fluctuations. Mean square fluctuations. Gaussian distribution of fluctuations.
  2. Fluctuations of volume, particle number, and density.
  3. Fluctuations of internal energy and temperature.
  4. Electrical fluctuations. Shot noise and Johnson–Nyquist thermal noise.

Part 5. Elements of Physical Kinetics

Lecture 14. Boltzmann Kinetic Equation

  1. Nonequilibrium states. Nonequilibrium distribution function.
  2. Boltzmann equation as a consequence of the Liouville theorem.
  3. Collision integral.
  4. Relaxation time approximation.
  5. Transport phenomena described by the Boltzmann equation.

Lecture 15. Transport Phenomena

  1. Mean free path. Effective cross section.
  2. Attenuation of molecular beams in gases.
  3. Viscosity and thermal conductivity of gases.
  4. Self-diffusion in gases.
  5. Rarefied gas phenomena. Effusion.

Part 6. Variable-Particle Systems. Phase Transitions

Lecture 16. Systems with Variable Particle Number. Chemical Potential

  1. Definition of chemical potential via thermodynamic potentials.
  2. Entropy increase in equilibration processes. Extremal properties of thermodynamic functions.
  3. Thermodynamic inequalities.

Lecture 17. Theory of Phase Transitions

  1. Phase concept. Phase equilibrium. First-order phase transitions.
  2. Triple point. Superheating and supercooling.
  3. Second-order phase transitions.
  4. Multicomponent systems. Gibbs phase rule.
  5. Chemical equilibrium in homogeneous systems. Law of mass action.
