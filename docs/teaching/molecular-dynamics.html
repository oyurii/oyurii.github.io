Course Description

This course focuses on modern molecular dynamics (MD) and ab initio simulation methods applied to the investigation of metals and alloys at the atomic scale. Special attention is given to MD algorithms, density functional theory (DFT), and hands-on experience with leading computational packages such as LAMMPS and Quantum ESPRESSO.

Students acquire practical skills in model construction, analysis of diffusion, mechanical and thermodynamic properties of materials, as well as electronic structure calculations. The course is aimed at developing competencies required for advanced scientific research in materials science and solid-state physics.

Course Objective

The objective of the course is to develop systematic theoretical knowledge and practical skills in applying molecular dynamics and ab initio quantum-mechanical methods to study atomic structure, physical properties, and evolutionary processes in metals and alloys.

Special emphasis is placed on the selection and application of modern interatomic potentials, DFT tools, and computational algorithms, as well as on the interpretation of simulation results for solving contemporary problems in solid-state physics and materials science.

Learning Outcomes

Competencies

Upon completion of the course, PhD students will be able to:

  • Understand fundamental principles of molecular dynamics (MD) and ab initio methods, including density functional theory (DFT).
  • Select and parameterize interatomic potentials for metals and alloys and assess their validity.
  • Perform classical MD simulations using LAMMPS.
  • Conduct DFT calculations using Quantum ESPRESSO.
  • Apply ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) for modeling atomic motion at finite temperatures.
  • Integrate MD and DFT results through multiscale and machine-learning approaches.
  • Critically analyze and interpret simulation results.

Learning Results

Students will be able to:

  • Explain physical principles underlying MD and ab initio methods.
  • Justify the choice of interatomic potentials and simulation parameters.
  • Construct atomic models including defects and surfaces.
  • Analyze diffusion, mechanical, thermodynamic, and electronic properties.

Course Content

Lectures

Module 1. Introduction and Theoretical Foundations

  1. Introduction to Computational Materials Science History and role of modeling; ab initio, MD, Monte Carlo methods. Hours: 2

  2. Fundamental Atomic-Scale Equations Schrödinger equation; Born–Oppenheimer approximation. Hours: 2

  3. Quantum-Mechanical Calculation Methods DFT, pseudopotentials, exchange–correlation functionals. Hours: 2

Module 2. Interatomic Interactions

  1. Empirical Potentials for Metals EAM, MEAM, Finnis–Sinclair, Gupta potentials. Hours: 2

  2. Construction and Scaling of Potentials Alloy systems and validation strategies. Hours: 2

Module 3. Molecular Dynamics

  1. Fundamentals of Classical Molecular Dynamics Integration algorithms; statistical ensembles. Hours: 2

  2. Implementation of MD in Software Packages LAMMPS workflows and input structures. Hours: 2

  3. Modeling Physical Properties of Metals Elastic constants, diffusion, correlation functions. Hours: 2

  4. Crystal Defects and Their Dynamics Vacancies, dislocations, grain boundaries. Hours: 2

Module 4. Interaction with Environment

  1. Oxidation and Corrosion in MD Models Reactive force fields: ReaxFF, COMB. Hours: 2

  2. Surface Phenomena and Nanostructures Surface energy and clustering. Hours: 2

Module 5. Ab initio Dynamics and Multiphysics

  1. Ab initio Molecular Dynamics (AIMD) Car–Parrinello MD. Hours: 2

  2. Combining DFT and MD QM/MM and machine-learning potentials. Hours: 2

Module 6. Practical Applications

  1. Modeling Structural and Kinetic Processes in Alloys Phase transitions and diffusion. Hours: 2

  2. Modeling Radiation Damage in Materials Displacement cascades. Hours: 2
